Andres Klene

My full name is Andres Klene-Sanchez or Andrés Klene Sánchez, depending on which government you ask.

In 2024 I completed my MMath in Mathematics at the University of Oxford. Here's a CV. Email address: andres.klene⊗outlook.com

I'm currently applying for PhDs in mathematics. Meanwhile, I'm teaching A-Level Mathematics and Further Mathematics at Bacon's College, London.

interests

My undergraduate background is in symplectic geometry. I'm currently learning some (∞-)category theory, higher/derived geometry, and various applications in theoretical physics. I'm writing some of this down in a set of notes called "Doodles of a Mouse" (after Johnstone's Sketches of an Elephant).

dissertations

My Part C (master's) dissertation was on Geometric quantisation and Hamiltonian cobordism, supervised by Andrew Dancer. Before that, I wrote a Part B (bachelor's) essay on Pseudo-holomorphic curves in the complex projective plane , under Guillem Cazassus.

summer projects

In 2023, I did a summer project on symplectic topology and mirror symmetry, funded by the EPSRC and supervised by Jason Lotay. I studied triangulated A∞-categories, symplectic Lefschetz fibrations, and toric varieties. At the end, I tried to verify homological mirror symmetry for the one-point blowup of ℙ3, producing a poster for an EPSRC event.

In 2022, I did a summer project on fractal geometry and stochastic analysis, funded by Hertford College and supervised by Ben Hambly. I wrote a short report to prove to Hertford College that I hadn't been stealing their money.

miscellaneous

I was a founding member of the TeXromancers, an online team dedicated to re-typesetting classic mathematical texts (in fact, I came up with the name!). I worked on their first two books: Stable Homotopy and Generalised Homology and Notes on Differential Geometry. Sadly, the group has since disbanded.

I also wrote an expository article called Associative Metrics for the University of Oxford's mathematics society, The Invariants. To be honest, it's not very good; I agreed to write the article under the impression that I had found a simpler answer to this MathOverflow question, which turned out to be nonsense.
