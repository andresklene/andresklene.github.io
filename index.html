    Welcome! I recently completed my MMath in Mathematics at the University of Oxford.

    A couple of years ago, I wrote my third-year essay on pseudo-holomorphic curves. I was very lucky to be supervised by Dr. Guillem Cazassus, who invited me to learn some Floer theory at the BTW3 workshop in Marseille. That summer, I did a summer project on symplectic topology under Prof. Jason Lotay, where I studied Fukaya categories associated to Lefschetz fibrations and their role in the homological mirror symmetry of Fano varieties. I wrote my master's dissertation on geometric quantisation.

    I am currently learning about gerbes and higher parallel transport!

    Writing

    Here is a list of the written work I completed at (and directly after) university. Some have received post-edits to fix typos and provide clarity. There is no claim to any originality of content.

    Undergraduate theses

    Geometric quantisation and Hamiltonian cobordism
    Part C Dissertation (master's thesis) supervised by Prof. Andrew Dancer.
    Topics: Hamiltonian group actions, virtual Hilbert representations, Hamiltonian cobordism.

    Pseudo-holomorphic curves in the complex projective plane
    Part B Essay (bachelor's thesis) supervised by Dr. Guillem Cazassus.
    Topics: geometry of 4-manifolds, index theory, Gromov compactness.

    Lecture notes

    I am writing the official notes for the Oxford course on commutative algebra lectured by my college tutor Prof. Dawid Kielak. These will be based on notes by the previous lecturer Prof. Damian Rössler, with input from Gathmann's notes and various student suggestions.

    Miscellaneous

    I was a founding member of the TeXromancers, an online team dedicated to re-typesetting classic mathematical texts (in fact, I came up with the name!). I worked on their first two books: Stable Homotopy and Generalised Homology by J. F. Adams and Notes on Differential Geometry by Noel Nicks. Sadly, they are now disbanded.

    I also wrote an expository article called Associative Metrics for the University of Oxford's mathematics society, The Invariants. To be honest, it's not very good; I agreed to write the article under the impression that I had proved a simpler answer to this MathOverflow question, which turned out to be nonsense.
