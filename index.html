My full name is Andres Klene-Sanchez or Andrés Klene Sánchez, depending on which government you ask.

In 2024 I completed my MMath in Mathematics at the University of Oxford. Here's a CV.

I'm currently applying for PhDs in pure mathematics and mathematical physics. Meanwhile, I'm teaching A Level Mathematics and Further Mathematics at my old secondary school.

current interests

I'm currently learning category theory, higher/derived geometry, and applications in theoretical physics. I'm writing some of this down in a set of notes called ``Doodles of a Mouse'' (after Johnstone's Sketches of an Elephant).

dissertations

My Part C (master's) dissertation was on Geometric quantisation and Hamiltonian cobordism, supervised by Andrew Dancer. Before that, I wrote a Part B (bachelor's) essay on Pseudo-holomorphic curves in the complex projective plane , under Guillem Cazassus.

summer projects

During the summer of 2023, I did a research project on symplectic topology and mirror symmetry, funded by the EPSRC and supervised by Jason Lotay. I studied triangulated A∞-categories, symplectic Lefschetz fibrations, and toric varieties. Towards the end of the project I attempted to verify (one side of) homological mirror symmetry for CP3 blown up at a point, producing a poster (hosted at Jason's website).

During the summer of 2022, I did a research project on fractal geometry and stochastic analysis, titled, funded by Hertford College and supervised by Ben Hambly. I studied a class of asymmetric diffusion-type processes on plane fractals, numerically answering one of Ben's conjectures in the negative.

miscellaneous

I was a founding member of the TeXromancers, an online team dedicated to re-typesetting classic mathematical texts (in fact, I came up with the name!). I worked on their first two books: Stable Homotopy and Generalised Homology and Notes on Differential Geometry. Sadly, the group has since disbanded.

I also wrote an expository article called Associative Metrics for the University of Oxford's mathematics society, The Invariants. To be honest, it's not very good; I agreed to write the article under the impression that I had proved a simpler answer to this MathOverflow question, which turned out to be nonsense.
