What is higher geometry?

Here are some words about the main ideas of higher differential geometry.

Higher geometry = geometry $+$ homotopy theory

Once upon a time (the 1930s) a branch of topology was born; it was called homotopy theory. Just as topologists forget the geometry of an object and remember its underlying topological space, so homotopy theorists forget the topological space and remember only its underlying "anima". For example, a circle and a cylinder have the same anima; each Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$ is sent to the anima of a point. Extracting the anima seems like a very violent thing to do, but in fact many basic algebraic invariants (such as the Euler characteristic, homotopy groups, homology and cohomology), … only see the anima of a space.

Time has passed, and a flipped perspective has emerged, whereby anima themselves are the objects of interest, and they are merely modelled, or presented, by topological spaces. Homotopy theory is really the study of anima.

Mathematics in the mid-20th century saw the homological revolution. The vague idea is that, when studying a complicated object, it is productive to replace (or "resolve") it by a sequence of simpler objects. Recently (in the last 20 years or so) the homological revolution has seen a second wave, the "homotopical revolution", which has seen ideas from homotopy theory injected into many areas of pure mathematics. Ideas from homological algebra have been reconceptualised and extended to non-linear settings, such as geometry.

Higher geometry is the synthesis of geometry and homotopy theory. Spaces in higher geometry are called stacks. All old smooth spaces are stacks, but there are new spaces which feel a little weird: Rather than have a set of points, they have an anima of points.

What is a stack, really?

It may be easier to start with a simpler question: What is a principal $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle on a manifold $M$? There are two ways of thinking about this concept:

  • Heuristically, we can think of a $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle as a smooth assignment of a $\mathrm{U}(1)$-torsor $P_x$ to each point $x \in M$. We can imagine this as something like a smooth map \begin{align*} T_{(-)} \colon M &\to \mathrm{Tors} \\ x &\mapsto T_x, \end{align*} where $\mathrm{Tors}$ is some smooth collection of $\mathrm{U(1)}-torsors. But this is just intuition.
  • Formally, a principal $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle is a big space $P$ together with a projection $\pi \colon P \to M$ such that each fibre $\pi^{-1}(x)$ is a $\mathrm{U}(1)$, which is locally trivial...
To me, the point of higher geometry is to legitimise the first perspective. If you're willing to sit through the formalisation of this in terms of category theory and so on, then at the end, there is a geometric object called the "stack of $\mathrm{U}(1)$-torsors", which is denoted $\mathbf{B} \mathrm{U}(1)$. Then a principal $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle on $M$ is really the same thing as a smooth map $M \to \mathbf{B} \mathrm{U}(1)$.
