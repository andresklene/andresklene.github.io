Homotopical geometry

From topology to homotopy theory

Once upon a time (the 1930s) a branch of topology was born, called homotopy theory. Topologists forget the geometry of an object and only remember its underlying topological space. In the same way, homotopy theorists forget the topological space and remember only its underlying anima, or homotopy type. The word 'anima' comes from the idea that "the functor from topological spaces to their homotopy category extracts something like the soul of a space: it only remembers data independent of any worldly representation in terms of physical points."

Time has passed, and a flipped perspective has emerged, whereby anima themselves are the objects of interest, which are merely modelled, or presented, by topological spaces. Homotopy theory is really the study of anima, and it has even been suggested that its origins in algebraic topology are something of a historical accident. The homotopical revolution

In the 1950s, mathematics saw a conceptual unification of many ad hoc ideas in algebra, which came to be called the homological revolution. The vague idea, which homological algebra formalises, is the replacement of a complicated algebraic structure by a sequence of simpler structures. Since the turn of the 21st century, the homological revolution has seen a second wave, the homotopical revolution, in which the homological framework has been extended to other areas, such as geometry.

Higher geometry is the synthesis of geometry and homotopy theory. Spaces in higher geometry are called stacks. All old smooth spaces are stacks, but there are new spaces which feel a little weird: Rather than have a set of points, they have an anima of points.

What is a stack, really?

It may be easier to start with a simpler question: What is a principal $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle on a manifold $M$? There are two ways of thinking about this concept:

  • Heuristically, we can think of a $G$-bundle as a smooth assignment of a $G$-torsor $P_x$ to each point $x \in M$. We can imagine this as something like a smooth map \begin{align*} T_{(-)} \colon M &\to \mathrm{Tors} \\ x &\mapsto T_x, \end{align*} where $\mathrm{Tors}$ is some smooth collection of $G$-torsors. But this is just intuition.
  • Formally, a principal $G$-bundle is a big space $P$ together with a projection $\pi \colon P \to M$ such that each fibre $\pi^{-1}(x)$ is a $G$-torsor, which is locally trivial...
To me, the point of higher geometry is to legitimise the first perspective. If you're willing to sit through the formalisation of this in terms of category theory and so on, then at the end, there is a geometric object called the "stack of $G$-torsors", which is denoted $\mathbf{B} G$. Then a principal $G$-bundle on $M$ is really the same thing as a smooth map $M \to \mathbf{B} G$.
