higher geometry

This exposition is nothing if not a work in progress; if you manage to find this webpage, don't tell your friends! Anyway, the point is that $$\text{higher geometry} = \text{geometry} + \text{homotopy theory}.$$ We will explain each summand individually.

homotopy theory

Homotopy theory started as a subfield of topology. Its practicioners only care about a topological space up to homotopy equivalence, which is to say they forget everything except its underlying homotopy type, or anima. The word 'anima' (plural: anima) comes from the idea that "the functor from topological spaces to their homotopy category extracts something like the soul of a space: it only remembers data independent of any worldly representation in terms of physical points." Homotopy theory is the study of anima.

Traditionally, one describes anima via their incarnations—like in the movies when an evil spirit possesses a mortal body so that we, the audience, can follow the plot. At a technical level, this entails the use of model categories. This machinery also encodes a mechanism of replacing/resolving bad incarnations with well-behaved ones—like when the evil spirit switches from a weak body to a strong one.

The shinier, modern approach is to work directly with the associated \(\infty\)-category of anima. That this is possible and productive is thanks to the recent foundations of $\infty$-category theory, which are due to many people—perhaps most notably Jacob Lurie. My personal experience is that one generally follows the creed "think globally, compute locally," which translates to "think $\infty$-categorically, compute model-categorically."

What is a stack?

It may be easier to start with a simpler question: What is a principal $\mathrm{U}(1)$-bundle on a manifold $M$? There are two ways of thinking about this concept:

  • Heuristically, we can think of a $G$-bundle as a smooth assignment of a $G$-torsor $P_x$ to each point $x \in M$. We can imagine this as something like a smooth map \begin{align*} T_{(-)} \colon M &\to \mathrm{Tors} \\ x &\mapsto T_x, \end{align*} where $\mathrm{Tors}$ is some smooth collection of $G$-torsors. But this is just intuition.
  • Formally, a principal $G$-bundle is a big space $P$ together with a projection $\pi \colon P \to M$ such that each fibre $\pi^{-1}(x)$ is a $G$-torsor, which is locally trivial...
To me, the point of higher geometry is to legitimise the first perspective. If you're willing to sit through the formalisation of this in terms of category theory and so on, then at the end, there is a geometric object called the "stack of $G$-torsors", which is denoted $\mathbf{B} G$. Then a principal $G$-bundle on $M$ is really the same thing as a smooth map $M \to \mathbf{B} G$.

Gauge theories as sigma-models

One nice consequence of the classifying stack-perspective is that the field configurations in $G$-gauge theory—connections on principal bundles—are maps into $\mathbf{B}_\nabla G$. In this way, gauge theories can be viewed as sigma-models, notwithstanding the fact (which I heard from a physicist on the street) that, at least in certain circles, the term 'sigma-model' is usually reserved for a much narrower class of theories.
